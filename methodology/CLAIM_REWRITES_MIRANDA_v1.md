## REWRITE 1 — HI-1: Slave Voyages Embarked vs. Disembarked

**ORIGINAL (draft):**
> "12.5 million Africans were transported to the Americas."

**PROBLEM:** "Transported" conflates two distinct figures. 12.5 million embarked on slave ships. 10.7 million survived to reach the Americas. The gap — nearly 1.8 million people — represents deaths at sea. Conflating these understates the mortality of the Middle Passage and creates a factual vulnerability any reviewer will catch.

**APPROVED REWRITE:**
> "Between 1514 and 1866, approximately 12.5 million Africans were forced onto slave ships. Nearly 1.8 million did not survive the crossing. Roughly 10.7 million arrived in the Americas — the survivors of a systematic death machine (Slave Voyages Database, slavevoyages.org, 2023 edition)."

**Alternative (condensed, for tables/captions):**
> "~12.5 million Africans embarked; ~10.7 million reached the Americas. ~1.8 million died at sea (Slave Voyages DB, 2023)."

**Footnote:**
> Slave Voyages Database, 2023 edition. slavevoyages.org. Figures represent voyages 1514–1866 with documented records. Estimates for undocumented voyages suggest total embarkations may be higher.

## REWRITE 2 — H-3 / CD-5: Cancer Alley Geography

**ORIGINAL (draft):**
> "Cancer Alley is 85% Black."

**PROBLEM:** The 85% figure applies to fence-line census tracts — communities immediately adjacent to industrial facilities — not to the parish as a whole. At the parish level, Black population share ranges from 22–57%. Citing 85% without the geographic specification invites immediate factual challenge from reviewers familiar with Louisiana parish demographics.

**APPROVED REWRITE:**
> "The communities bearing the highest industrial pollution burden in the Louisiana petrochemical corridor — known as Cancer Alley — are disproportionately Black. At the fence-line level, the census tracts immediately adjacent to industrial facilities in St. James Parish (Districts 4–5) are 65 to 94 percent Black (ACS 2022). These are the communities breathing the emissions. The parish-level Black population share of 44 percent does not capture where the burden lands."

**Alternative (condensed, single sentence):**
> "At the fence-line census tract level — the communities directly adjacent to industrial facilities — the Black population share in St. James Parish, Louisiana ranges from 65 to 94 percent (ACS 2022, St. James Parish Districts 4–5)."

**Footnote:**
> U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2022 5-Year Estimates. Geographic unit: census tracts in St. James Parish, Louisiana, Districts 4–5. Parish-level Black population share is approximately 44%. Fence-line exposure geography following methodology of Mikati et al. (2018) and Bullard (2000).

## REWRITE 3 — ED-1: NAEP Gap and Parity Timeline

**ORIGINAL (draft):**
> "The Black-white achievement gap has narrowed from 30 to 20 points. At current rates, parity would be reached in 64 years."

**PROBLEM:** NAEP 2022 shows the 8th-grade reading gap at 24 points, not 20. The parity timeline from a linear trend projection is over 120 years, not 64. Both errors understate the severity of the problem — and both are easily checked by any education researcher.

**APPROVED REWRITE:**
> "The Black-white gap in 8th-grade reading scores has narrowed — from approximately 30 points in 1992 to 24 points in 2022 (NAEP Main Assessment). That is 6 points of progress in three decades. At that convergence rate, parity is more than a century away. The gap is closing. It is not closing fast enough to constitute a solution."

**Alternative (condensed):**
> "The Black-white 8th-grade reading gap stands at 24 percentage points (NAEP 2022), down from approximately 30 points in 1992. Linear projection to parity exceeds 120 years."

**Footnote:**
> National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) Main Assessment, 2022. 8th-grade reading, average scale score by race/ethnicity. National Center for Education Statistics (NCES). Data accessed via nationsreportcard.gov.

## REWRITE 4 — CD-6: Humphreys County Poverty Rate

**PROBLEM:** The draft uses 37% (ACS 2019 vintage, 36.4% all-persons, rounds to 37%). The current ACS 2022 figure is 32.1%. Neither is wrong — but using the older figure without a vintage lock invites a factual challenge that distracts from the argument.

**BOARD RECOMMENDATION:** Use ACS 2022 (32.1%) with the child poverty figure (55.0%) — the current data is more defensible and the child poverty number is more powerful.

**APPROVED REWRITE (recommended — current vintage):**
> "In Humphreys County, 32 percent of residents live below the poverty line — and 55 percent of children (ACS 2022)."

**Alternative (keeps 37% with vintage lock):**
> "In the years before the COVID pandemic, 36.4 percent of Humphreys County residents lived below the poverty line (ACS 2015–2019 5-Year Estimate). As of 2022, that figure stands at 32.1 percent — a reduction that reflects neither prosperity nor policy but the statistical artifact of population contraction in a county that has lost a third of its residents since 1980."

**Footnote (recommended version):**
> U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 2022 5-Year Estimates (2018–2022). Table B17001, poverty status by sex and age. FIPS: 28053. Child poverty figure: Table B17001, under 18 years. nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/coi.

## REWRITE 5 — HO-3: Section 3 HUD "$4.5 Billion Gap"

**ORIGINAL (draft):**
> "Section 3 HUD obligations represent a $4.5 billion cumulative gap."

**PROBLEM:** No published source exists for this figure. It is internally derived from an unpublished E5 Enclave calculation. Citing an unverifiable aggregate dollar figure in external academic or grant documents exposes the entire paper to credibility challenge. Replace with the verifiable HUD OIG finding, which is stronger precisely because it comes from HUD's own inspector general.

**APPROVED REWRITE:**
> "Section 3 of the Housing and Urban Development Act of 1968 requires that HUD-funded construction provide employment and contracting opportunities to low-income residents of the communities being developed. A 2013 audit by HUD's own Office of Inspector General found that 53 percent of public housing authorities did not file the required Section 3 reports — making the program's theoretical benefit largely unverifiable (HUD OIG, 2013). The gap between statutory mandate and documented delivery has never been closed."

**Alternative (condensed):**
> "HUD OIG (2013) found 53% of public housing authorities did not file required Section 3 reports — the mechanism for documenting whether low-income residents received the employment and contracting opportunities the law requires."

**Footnote:**
> U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, Office of Inspector General. *Audit of HUD's Section 3 Program.* 2013-AT-0003. March 28, 2013. Available: hudoig.gov.
